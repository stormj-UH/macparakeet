# Biometric Privacy Brief: Persistent Speaker Profiles

Stored voice embeddings for cross-meeting speaker labeling should be treated as biometric data when they are created to recognize a person. GDPR defines biometric data as personal data from technical processing of physical, physiological, or behavioral characteristics that allow or confirm unique identification ([GDPR Art. 4(14)](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-4-gdpr/)); Art. 9 then prohibits processing "biometric data for the purpose of uniquely identifying a natural person" unless an exception applies, usually explicit consent for this feature ([GDPR Art. 9](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-9-gdpr/)). BIPA is even more direct: "biometric identifier" includes a "voiceprint," and "biometric information" includes information based on a biometric identifier used to identify someone; Sec. 15 requires written notice of collection/storage, purpose and term, a written release, a public retention/destruction policy, no sale/profit, and reasonable protection ([740 ILCS 14](https://law.justia.com/codes/illinois/2024/chapter-740/act-740-ilcs-14/)). White Castle is the warning case: the Illinois Supreme Court held in 2023 that each unlawful scan/transmission could accrue separately, though Illinois later amended BIPA to limit repeated same-method collection/disclosure of the same biometric to one recovery ([Cothron v. White Castle](https://law.justia.com/cases/illinois/supreme-court/2023/128004.html); [2024 BIPA text](https://law.justia.com/codes/illinois/2024/chapter-740/act-740-ilcs-14/)). Voice-specific risk is not theoretical: a 2026 Microsoft Teams class action alleges real-time transcription captured pitch/tone/timbre voice data for speaker identification without BIPA-compliant notice and consent ([Windows Central](https://www.windowscentral.com/microsoft/microsoft-teams/teams-class-action-lawsuit-illinois-biometric-privacy)). Otter's own policy calls out "Speaker Identification / Biometric Information" and says it generates speaker identification to recognize and tag speakers ([Otter privacy](https://otter.ai/privacy-policy)).

If the app vendor truly never receives audio, transcripts, embeddings, sync copies, telemetry payloads, or support bundles containing profiles, the vendor has a stronger argument that it is not the GDPR controller/processor and not the BIPA entity that "collects," "captures," "obtains," or possesses the biometric. The user or organization enrolling other participants is the party deciding purpose and means locally. That said, do not overstate "no vendor risk": GDPR's household exemption excludes a natural person's purely personal/household activity, but not professional/commercial use, and Recital 18 says GDPR still applies to controllers/processors that provide means for personal/household processing ([GDPR Art. 2](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-2-gdpr/), [Recital 18](https://gdpr-info.eu/recitals/no-18/)). Personal notes from family/friends may fit the household exemption; workplace, customer, recruiting, sales, board, or recorded team meetings likely do not. In those settings the employer/host is the controller and needs a GDPR lawful basis plus an Art. 9 condition, and may need a DPIA for high-risk or large-scale special-category processing ([GDPR Art. 35](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-35-gdpr/)). BIPA has no neat household carveout and defines "private entity" broadly, so Illinois enrollment is the sharpest user-risk case.

Shipped products frame speaker ID as opt-in identity personalization plus consent/permissions. Otter says users may provide recordings, Otter generates speaker identification, and users should make sure they have necessary permissions before sharing third-party personal information ([Otter privacy](https://otter.ai/privacy-policy)). Zoom says enhanced features may process unique physical characteristics, including "voiceprint," only if the user elects to enable the feature and provides requisite consent, with deletion when no longer needed or after two years since last interaction; its current privacy statement also says it does not use customer audio/video/chat-like content to train Zoom or third-party AI models ([Zoom privacy](https://www.zoom.com/en/trust/privacy/privacy-statement/)). Google Voice Match requires the user to link an account, turn on Voice Match, and teach Assistant their voice; Google says a unique voice model is created, can be removed from devices/home/all devices, and comparison data is deleted immediately after processing ([Google Voice Match](https://support.google.com/assistant/answer/9071681?hl=en)). Alexa's developer docs similarly separate account user from recognized speaker and say Alexa sends no personally identifying information about either without customer consent; 2025 reporting noted that disabling saved recordings can limit Alexa Voice ID-like personalization ([Alexa personalization docs](https://developer.amazon.com/en-US/docs/alexa/custom-skills/add-personalization-to-your-skill.html), [Lifewire](https://www.lifewire.com/is-alexa-safe-to-use-11707929)). The Zoom/Otter controversies are mostly about cloud meeting content, bots, AI training, and notice, not merely speaker labels; the lesson is visible consent and deletion controls, not silent background enrollment.

Mitigations that matter: keep profiles local-only and encrypted; make enrollment explicit and per-speaker, never automatic from ambient meetings; require the user to confirm they have permission/consent for each named profile; provide per-profile delete/retrain and a global disable; avoid cloud sync/backups by default; provide a BIPA/GDPR notice template and retention language for business users; and consider disabling the feature in Illinois unless there is a real written-release workflow. Be careful with "embeddings are not reversible to audio." It is fair to say the app does not store raw enrollment audio and a small embedding is not a normal audio file. It is not fair to call it anonymous or harmless: voiceprint privacy research treats voiceprint as biometric ([Voice-Indistinguishability](https://arxiv.org/abs/2004.07442)), and x-vector anonymization work shows speaker-embedding protections can be overestimated and partially reversed by attackers ([Vocoder drift](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.02892)).

Bottom line: shipping is probably acceptable risk for an indie local-first vendor if the feature is framed as sensitive biometric speaker profiling and built as an opt-in local tool, but the risk is split: vendor risk is mainly product-design, consumer-protection, and "did you actually collect/process anything?" risk; user risk is the direct GDPR/BIPA risk of enrolling other people's voiceprints. The three must-do safeguards are: (1) off-by-default, user-initiated per-speaker enrollment with an explicit "I have permission/consent" gate; (2) local encrypted storage with per-profile delete and no vendor/cloud access, sync, telemetry, or support export by default; and (3) regional/workplace guardrails: Illinois disable-or-written-release mode, business-user notice templates, retention/deletion docs, and clear wording that participants' voice profiles are biometric data.
